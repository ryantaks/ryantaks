Project 1: Beyond Irresponsibility — Understanding the Complexity of College Student Financial Behavior

February 13, 2026

This project asked us to explore an issue relevant to our field through a peer-reviewed research lens. Writing this paper pushed me to engage seriously with academic sources, understand how financial literacy is studied empirically, and challenge the assumptions embedded in how people talk about student debt. The FSU library databases were essential resources for locating sources, and the process of building the annotated bibliography helped me understand how individual studies speak to and complicate each other. Through writing this paper, I developed a stronger ability to synthesize multiple scholarly perspectives into a cohesive argument.

The common perception of college-age individuals is to view them as bad with money. However, when we examine this issue through peer-reviewed research, it loses a lot of its appeal. College student financial troubles occur for many reasons other than laziness or immaturity; there are objective gaps in the student's financial literacy, psychological influences on the student's behaviour, and differences between objective and subjective knowledge about finances.

Reflection

Reflecting on ENC 2135

As my time in Research, Genre, and Context comes to an end, the changes in my writing throughout this course — though sometimes subtle — are clear to me now in a way they weren't at the start. Coming into this class as a freshman, I had a surface-level understanding of what research writing meant. I thought it was mostly about finding sources and citing them correctly. This course showed me how much more is involved.

Analyzing genre taught me to look beneath the type of text and think about the rhetorical situation shaping it. A peer-reviewed article and a pamphlet can address the same topic, but the audience, purpose, and design choices make them entirely different genres. Learning to ask why a piece looks and sounds the way it does has changed how I read everything — not just academic writing.

Project 1 was the most challenging and rewarding part of the course. Researching college student financial behavior pushed me to engage with real empirical studies and understand how researchers argue with evidence, not just opinion. Building the annotated bibliography was new territory — I had to go beyond summarizing and actually evaluate how each source contributed to a larger scholarly conversation. I also had to reckon with the fact that sources sometimes contradict each other, and that complexity is not a weakness in an argument; it's often its strength.

Working on the email to FSU administration as an artifact gave me a different kind of challenge. Taking research-based insights and shaping them for a professional, institutional audience required me to shift my register, sharpen my purpose, and think about what a real reader would need to be persuaded. It reinforced that writing is always shaped by who you're writing for and what you want them to do.

In conclusion, this course has pushed me to become a more thoughtful, purposeful writer. I leave ENC 2135 with a better understanding of how research, genre, and audience work together — and with writing skills I know I'll carry into my Political Science coursework and beyond.
